01 / What this can help with

Common symptoms
we see and work to reduce.

Therapeutic massage can reduce tension, ease pain, and support better shoulder function. It is not a cure for structural issues, but targeted manual therapy helps many clients with:

  • Shoulder pain or aching that returns after rest
  • Pain around the shoulder blade or between the blades
  • Tightness or limited reach overhead or across the body
  • Clicking or catching with shoulder movement
  • Neck and upper trap tension that moves with the shoulder
  • Chest and front-shoulder tightness from forward posture
  • Upper arm aching or heaviness
  • Pain that worsens with desk work, driving, or phone use
02 / Why it keeps coming back

The pain returns because
the pattern stays.

Shoulder pain that keeps coming back usually has more going on than a single tight muscle. Common contributors:

Posture and desk load
Hours of forward head and rounded shoulder posture compress the joint and put steady load on the surrounding muscles. The pain eases with rest, then comes back because the posture doesn't change.
Tight chest and front shoulder
Pec minor and pec major pull the shoulder forward and inward. When these stay short, the muscles behind the shoulder work twice as hard to stabilize every movement.
Dominant-side overuse
Training, sport, or a mouse-heavy workday loads one shoulder far more than the other. The overloaded side builds tension patterns that don't reset between sessions.
Mid-back stiffness
A restricted thoracic spine forces the shoulder to make up range it was never meant to provide. The shoulder moves more, fatigues faster, and hurts sooner.
Compensated movement
Once a shoulder has been sore for a while, other muscles shift how they move to protect it. Those compensated patterns often persist even after the original irritation settles.
03 / How treatment works

A structured session,
built around your pattern.

Every appointment starts with a short assessment of shoulder range, posture, and where the tension chain begins. From there, your therapist works through a sequence tailored to your presentation. Pressure is firm, specific, and always calibrated to your tolerance.

  1. Step 01 ~5 min

    Assessment

    Shoulder range of motion, scapular movement, and postural assessment. We map which muscles are carrying excess load and where compensation is happening before we begin hands-on work.

  2. Step 02 ~10 min

    Thoracic and neck release

    Upper traps, levator scapulae, scalenes, and mid-back extensors. A stiff thoracic spine and overloaded neck are often the reason a shoulder stays under load, so we address them before working the joint itself.

  3. Step 03 ~10 min

    Chest and anterior shoulder

    Pec minor, pec major, and anterior deltoid. Releasing the forward pull on the shoulder changes the mechanical environment the whole joint operates in and gives the muscles behind it a chance to recover.

  4. Step 04 ~15 min

    Periscapular and rotator cuff work

    Rhomboids, infraspinatus, teres minor, and subscapularis. These are the muscles doing the actual recurring work. Slow, specific pressure on each, working through the full range of the shoulder blade.

  5. Step 05 ~5 min

    Home plan

    One or two targeted mobility cues and a posture reset you will actually use between visits. Short and specific, not a twelve-exercise handout.

04 / Progress and frequency

Realistic expectations
for your progress.

Some clients feel a meaningful difference after a single session. Others, especially those with long-standing tension patterns, dominant-side overuse, or chronic postural load, benefit from several sessions over a few weeks. Your therapist will recommend a plan based on what we find during assessment and how your body responds between visits.

For shoulder pain with a structural component, such as significant rotator cuff involvement or post-surgical recovery, physiotherapy or specialist care may be the right primary path. We will tell you directly if that is the case, and help you find the right next step.

06 / Things people ask first

Clear answers, no jargon.

Does it hurt?

Pressure is firm and specific, but always calibrated to your tolerance. Your therapist works below the threshold where the body starts to guard, and you are always in control of the pressure.

How many sessions will I need?

It depends on how long the pattern has been building and what is contributing to it. Some clients feel meaningful change after one session. Others benefit from a series of a few sessions over a few weeks. Your therapist will recommend a plan based on the assessment.

Can I keep training while I come in?

Yes. If you are dealing with post-session soreness, lighter intensity for 24 hours is a good idea. For an ongoing training schedule alongside a massage series, your therapist can help you sequence around your program.

Should I see a physiotherapist instead?

Massage and physio serve different roles and often work well together. If your shoulder pain has a structural or neurological component, we will flag it during assessment. Many clients work with both and find the combination effective.

Do you direct bill insurance?

Yes. All four locations direct bill most major Canadian insurance providers. Coverage and copay vary by plan, so check with your insurer for your specific benefits.
